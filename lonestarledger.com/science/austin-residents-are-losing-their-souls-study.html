Peer-Reviewed Study Confirms: Austin Is Slowly Eating Its Residents' Souls

A 14-year longitudinal study finds that every year spent in the Texas capital costs residents measurable soul-mass — and the losses accelerate sharply after the five-year mark. Researchers point to one culprit: the bats.

By Marlene Atwood-Pryce, Metaphysical Health Correspondent  |  Published 6:14 AM CT  |  8 min read fXin✉

AUSTIN — For decades, the nightly emergence of bats from beneath the Congress Avenue Bridge has drawn tourists, photographers, and the occasional bewildered conventioneer. Now, a study published this week in the Journal of Metaphysical Epidemiology suggests the spectacle may have a darker dimension: the bats, researchers say, are feeding on the souls of the people who live here.

The five-year investigation, led by the Barton Institute for Applied Metaphysical Research, tracked 4,200 Austin-area residents and measured what the authors call "ambient soul retention" using a proprietary instrument the team describes only as "a refined dowsing apparatus." The findings were consistent, the authors write, "and frankly upsetting."

Two percent a year — then it gets worse

According to the study, the average resident loses roughly 2% of their soul per year for the first five years of residence. That much, lead author Dr. Hollis Vane noted, is "survivable, even charming." The problem begins at year five.

"Once a resident crosses the five-year threshold, the annual loss doesn't just continue — it compounds," Vane said. "Each subsequent year removes an additional two percentage points on top of the previous year's rate. Year six you lose four percent. Year seven, six percent. By year ten you're shedding soul at a rate that, candidly, we'd recommend leaving over."

"We expected a slow drain. What we found was a cliff. The data doesn't lie, and neither, unfortunately, do the bats." — Dr. Hollis Vane, Barton Institute for Applied Metaphysical Research

As the chart shows, a resident who stays the full decade is projected to retain only about half of their original soul. Those approaching the 13-year mark are, per the model, "running on fumes, spiritually speaking." The institute stresses that 0% does not mean a person ceases to function — "they simply become very into queso and start every sentence with 'back when this was a cool city.'"

Why the bats?

The mechanism, the researchers propose, is dietary. Mexican free-tailed bats are known to consume thousands of tons of insects each year. But the Barton team argues the colony — one of the largest urban bat populations in North America — has adapted to supplement that diet with metaphysical material radiating off the dense human population downtown.

"Soul-stuff is, energetically speaking, extremely nutritious," explained co-author Dr. Priya Okafor. "It's the spiritual equivalent of a really good breakfast taco. The bats found a renewable source. We are the renewable source."

By the numbers
  • ~1.5 million bats beneath the Congress Avenue Bridge
  • 2% soul lost per year, years 0–5
  • +2 points/yr additional loss after year 5
  • ~50% soul remaining at the 10-year mark
  • ~0% projected by year 13
  • 0% reported soul loss in Round Rock (control group)

City response

A spokesperson for the city declined to confirm the findings but did not deny them, stating only that "Austin remains a vibrant, soul-forward community" and encouraging residents to "stay weird, while supplies last." The local tourism board has reportedly begun drafting signage for the city limits reading "Welcome to Austin — Mind Your Soul."

Newcomers, meanwhile, appear unbothered. "Two percent a year? I lose more than that in rent increases," said one recent transplant, who has lived in the city for fourteen months and "feels totally fine." Researchers note this is exactly what the early-stage data predicts.
